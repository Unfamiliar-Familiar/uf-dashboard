Advisory Council -- Five perspectives
Chief Strategy Officer
The proposal is competitively positioned. The heritage story is the moat -- no other applicant can manufacture 50 years of vinyl history.
+

The strategic positioning of this proposal is strong on three axes: differentiation (no other listening bar concept in Dubai, let alone one with immersive tech), credibility (Disc Jockey's 50-year heritage, Hybrid's blue-chip client list, Vinyl Souk's community track record), and alignment with Al Serkal's mission (interdisciplinary, community-first, culturally substantive).

The competitive advantage is real. Most Blank Space applicants will be individual artists, small collectives doing installations, or one-off exhibition proposals. A four-company collective proposing a fully programmed, revenue-generating cultural venue with 28 days of content is a different category of application. You are not competing against other listening bars -- you are competing against the typical Blank Space applicant, which skews toward visual art and short-run exhibitions. This proposal is bigger, more operational, and more community-oriented than what they usually receive.

The heritage angle is the strategic moat. "Born from Disc Jockey, Beirut's record store since 1970" is not a tagline -- it is provenance that cannot be replicated. In a region where cultural authenticity is increasingly valued, this lineage is your strongest card. Lead with it in the proposal. Do not bury it on slide 7.

Recommendations:

  • Lead the proposal with the heritage story -- Disc Jockey 1970, 90,000 records, now in Dubai
  • Position the 4-week activation as proof-of-concept for a permanent venue
  • Include a one-paragraph competitive analysis: "No listening bar exists in the GCC. The closest global comparisons (Tokyo, London) lack immersive tech."
  • Quantify community impact: expected footfall, F&B revenue for Al Serkal tenants, email signups, social impressions
Cultural Programming Director
The themed nights are strong, but the calendar needs artist performances, DJ school, and wellness programming to feel complete.
+

The five themed nights -- Midnight in Beirut, Disco Therapy, Jazz & Rain, Women in Music, Analog Love -- are emotionally compelling and well-conceived. Each creates a distinct world. The naming is strong. But a 4-week activation needs more than five recurring themes. The current programming reads as "five flavours of listening session." What's missing is variety of format -- not just variety of genre.

The proposal needs at least three additional programming formats to demonstrate the space's versatility: (1) Live artist performances -- invite local and regional musicians to play acoustic sets alongside vinyl. This is especially powerful for the "artists need a space to perform" community angle. (2) DJ school sessions with Flipside -- this is a public-facing educational element that Al Serkal loves. It shows the space is not just consumption but knowledge transfer. (3) Morning yoga or wellness classes using the space -- functional beverages (matcha, coffee), ambient sound, slow start. This fills the morning slot and shows the space works at all hours.

Additionally, open community listening sessions and gallery crossover programming should be on the calendar. Invite an Al Serkal gallery to curate visuals for one evening. Invite a cafe to do a tasting paired with vinyl. These cross-tenant activations are exactly what Al Serkal wants to see -- you are strengthening their ecosystem, not just occupying a warehouse.

Recommendations:

  • Add 2-3 confirmed live artist performances to the 4-week calendar (even "in discussion" names help)
  • Include 1 Flipside DJ school session per week -- public-facing, ticketed or free
  • Add 2 morning yoga/wellness sessions across the 4 weeks
  • Schedule 2 open community listening sessions (free, drop-in, no curation -- let the community bring records)
  • Plan 1 gallery crossover night -- partner with an existing Al Serkal gallery for visual curation
  • Build a full 28-day calendar, not just a list of themes. Day-by-day. Morning, afternoon, evening.
Community Impact Advisor
The community case is genuine, not performative. But spell it out -- Al Serkal needs to see the ripple effect on their tenants, not just on your audience.
+

The community impact argument is the second strongest element of this proposal, after the heritage story. Music bridges cultures -- that is not a platitude here, it is structurally true. A Lebanese vinyl archive playing Fairuz for an Emirati, Indian, Filipino, and European audience in Al Quoz is the kind of organic cultural exchange that Al Serkal was built for. This space does not need to "create" community -- it attracts people who already want connection and gives them a reason to sit together.

The key distinction to make in the proposal: this space drives repeat foot traffic, not one-time visits. A gallery exhibition gets visited once. A listening bar gets visited weekly. That is a fundamentally different value proposition for Al Serkal's ecosystem. Every return visit means another coffee from the cafe, another browse through the gallery next door, another meal at the restaurant. You are extending dwell time across the entire district.

The "not competing with existing tenants -- feeding them" framing is critical and must be explicit. The F&B integration (Al Serkal cafes serve inside the space) means existing tenants benefit directly. The gallery crossover nights mean existing galleries get new audiences. The DJ school means Flipside gets a venue. You are the rising tide, not a competitor for limited attention.

Recommendations:

  • Include a "Tenant Benefit Map" -- one paragraph showing how each type of Al Serkal tenant benefits (cafes, galleries, restaurants, studios)
  • Quantify: "We estimate 40-60 daily visitors during daytime, 50-80 per evening session. Each visitor spends an average of 2 hours in the Al Serkal precinct."
  • Mention the regional context: "Artists in the region need spaces to perform. This space provides that -- especially important during difficult times in Lebanon, Palestine, and the wider region."
  • Frame the space as a "cultural commons" -- open to all Al Serkal tenants for pop-ups, classes, and collaborations
Independent Advisory -- Ex-CEO, Al Serkal Avenue
This is one of the stronger Blank Space applications I would expect to see. But you need to understand what the committee is actually scoring -- and where most applicants fail.
+

What the selection committee actually looks for:

The Blank Space programme is not a grant. It is not a favour. It is Al Serkal investing warehouse space, WiFi, utilities, and marketing support in a project that enhances their brand positioning as the region's leading independent cultural district. The committee evaluates three things above all else: (1) Does this make Al Serkal more relevant? (2) Will this generate press, social media, and foot traffic? (3) Is this team capable of actually delivering what they promise? Most applicants fail on the third question. They have beautiful concepts and no operational plan.

What makes this proposal stand out:

Three things immediately separate this from the typical applicant pool. First, it is a collective -- not a solo artist asking for studio space. Collectives signal maturity, shared risk, and a broader community. Al Serkal has historically favoured collaborative proposals because they generate more activity and more cross-pollination. Second, the heritage story is extraordinary. Disc Jockey, founded 1970 in Beirut, 90,000 records, the oldest and largest collection in MENA -- that is a cultural asset, not a business pitch. The committee will remember this. Third, the immersive technology layer from Hybrid Xperience makes this press-worthy. Al Serkal needs content for their own marketing -- a listening bar with reactive projections gives them that content.

What the committee will scrutinise:

The committee will ask: "What happens on a Tuesday afternoon in Week 3?" If you cannot answer that with a specific programme, your proposal reads as aspirational, not operational. They have seen dozens of proposals that describe an incredible opening night and then have nothing for the remaining 27 days. Your 4-week calendar must be granular -- every day, morning through night. This is the single biggest differentiator between proposals that win and proposals that impress but lose.

They will also ask about the public-facing element. Blank Space requires it. Your evening ticketed sessions satisfy this, but the committee wants more than ticketed events -- they want elements that are free, accessible, and open to anyone walking through Al Serkal. Open studio days, community listening sessions, the daytime walk-in format, the DJ school sessions -- these are your public-facing answers. Make them prominent, not buried.

Common mistakes applicants make:

  • Leading with the concept instead of the community impact. Al Serkal does not fund cool ideas -- they fund ideas that strengthen their ecosystem. Lead with what you give, not what you get.
  • Treating the 10-slide limit as a creative constraint. It is a discipline test. If you cannot explain your project clearly in 10 slides, the committee doubts you can execute it clearly in 4 weeks.
  • Ignoring the "how will you use the space" question. This sounds obvious but most applicants describe what they will do, not how the physical space will be configured. Include a floor plan or spatial diagram -- even a rough one. Show furniture placement, tech setup, audience flow.
  • Not naming specific partners. "We will partner with local cafes" is weak. "We are in conversation with [specific Al Serkal cafe] to serve coffee in the space during daytime hours" is strong. Name names even if conversations are early.
  • Forgetting that Al Serkal has existing programming. Your proposal should complement, not conflict with, their calendar. Check what is already scheduled for your proposed dates.

The competitive landscape:

Blank Space typically receives 30-60 applications per cycle. Most are individual artists or small collectives proposing exhibitions, residencies, or single-event activations. A fully programmed, 28-day, multi-company, revenue-generating cultural venue is unusual and will stand out structurally. Your main competition is not other listening bars -- it is the 2-3 other strong proposals that also have operational depth, community integration, and a compelling cultural narrative. You need to be the one that feels most "ready to go" -- the committee wants to fund execution, not potential.

What would make the committee say yes immediately:

  • A complete 4-week calendar, day by day, with specific event names, formats, and times
  • At least one confirmed F&B partner from within Al Serkal (even informal confirmation)
  • A 60-second demo video or visual mockup from Hybrid Xperience showing the reactive projections
  • A spatial diagram showing how the warehouse will be configured (no structural changes -- they are strict about this)
  • A clear statement of who the single point of contact is (Peter) and how the collective makes decisions
  • Evidence of existing community: "Vinyl Souk has hosted X events with Y attendees. UF has Z subscribers and ships to W customers."

What would make them reject:

  • A proposal that reads as a commercial venture disguised as a cultural programme. If the ticket revenue and vinyl sales feel like the primary motive, the committee will pass. Lead with culture, heritage, and community. Revenue is a byproduct, not the goal.
  • Any hint of structural modifications to the space. Blank Space is explicit: no major changes. All your installations (projections, LED cube, furniture) must be movable. State this clearly.
  • A vague public-facing element. "The space will be open to the public" is not enough. Specific free events, open hours, community programming -- that is what they need to see.
  • No evidence the team can deliver. Include previous project examples, portfolio links, past event documentation.
Brand & Design Director
The story is there. The emotion is there. But the deck itself must be as curated as the crates -- 10 slides, no wasted space, every frame earns its place.
+

You have 10 slides. That is not a limitation -- it is the best thing about this format. A 10-slide deck forces clarity. Every slide must answer one question and nothing more. The storytelling arc should be: Heritage (who we are) → Gap (what's missing in Dubai) → Concept (the listening bar) → Collective (who delivers it) → Space (how it looks) → Programme (what happens for 4 weeks) → Community (who benefits) → Impact (what Al Serkal gains). That is 8 slides of substance and 2 slides for cover + closing.

The emotional anchor of the deck must be the heritage story. Slide 1 or 2 should feature Disc Jockey Beirut. If you have a photograph of the original store -- use it. If you have a photograph of the vinyl archive -- use it. The selection committee should feel the weight of 50 years of music curation before they even see the concept. This is what separates your proposal from every other "cool event idea" they receive.

Design language: use UF's brand guidelines. Beige (#FBF9F2), black (#23201F), butter (#FFF5C4). Inter Tight. Clean. Minimal. Let the photographs and the story do the work. Do not over-design. The deck should feel like the space it describes -- quiet, curated, confident. If the deck feels like a nightclub flyer, the committee will assume the space will too.

Recommended 10-slide structure:

  • Slide 1: Cover -- "The Listening Bar" / A proposal by [Collective name] for Al Serkal Blank Space
  • Slide 2: Heritage -- Disc Jockey, Beirut, 1970. The story. The archive. Photo if available.
  • Slide 3: The Collective -- 4 members, their roles, one line each. Logos.
  • Slide 4: The Concept -- Day mode / Night mode. Two columns. What it feels like.
  • Slide 5: The Space -- Floor plan or spatial diagram. Installations marked. "All movable, no structural changes."
  • Slide 6: Installations -- The 3 Hybrid pieces (projection, LED wall, Jukebox cube). Visual mockups.
  • Slide 7: 4-Week Activation Plan -- Calendar view. Every day mapped. Theme nights, open sessions, DJ school, yoga, artist performances.
  • Slide 8: Community Impact -- Who benefits. Tenant integration. Cultural bridge. Repeat foot traffic.
  • Slide 9: Public-Facing Element -- Free daytime access, open listening sessions, DJ school, community events.
  • Slide 10: The Ask -- What we need from Al Serkal. What Al Serkal gets. Contact: Peter / blankspace@alserkalavenue.ae.
