1970s

The Heritage

Sometime in the early 1970s, a man named Kamal Abou Hachem walked into a building on a narrow Beirut street, turned on the lights, and opened a record store. He called it Disc Jockey. There was no business plan, no market research, no investor pitch. There was just Kamal, a room full of vinyl, and the deep, irrational belief that people needed this music in their lives.

The store smelled like cardboard sleeves and dust and possibility. The first customers were friends, then neighbors, then strangers who heard about the place from other strangers. Kamal would pull a record from the shelf, drop the needle, and let the music do the selling. Fairuz in the morning. Abdel Halim in the afternoon. Um Kulthum when the sun went down and people lingered, not wanting to leave. The store became a living room for an entire neighborhood, then an entire city.

Over fifty years, Disc Jockey accumulated more than 90,000 records. Not as inventory—Kamal never thought of it that way. It was a living archive. A museum that breathed. Every record had a story: the pressing from Cairo that arrived during the civil war, the Lebanese jazz recordings that nobody else bothered to save, the complete discographies of artists the world was beginning to forget. Kamal remembered where every record came from. He could tell you the year, the label, the distributor, the customer who almost bought it but changed their mind.

Peter grew up in this world. As a boy, he sat behind the counter and watched his father talk to customers with a reverence that most people reserve for sacred things. The records were not products. They were family. Every scratched sleeve, every faded label, every warped pressing that still played if you held the tone arm just right—they were all part of something bigger than commerce. They were proof that someone cared enough to keep the music alive.

"This is where it all starts. Not with a business plan. With a father who loved music."

01
October 2025

The Trigger

Something happened with Joe and his dad that October. The details don't matter here—what matters is that it broke something inside Peter that had been holding together by force of will. He had just come off a wedding, emotionally spent, running on fumes and caffeine and the stubborn belief that if he just kept moving, the exhaustion wouldn't catch him. He was in clean-up mode. The kind of mode where you answer every email, pay every bill, apologize to every person you've been neglecting, and pretend that productivity is the same thing as healing.

But Peter doesn't collapse. That's not how he's wired. He takes the slap—life's slap, not a person's—and he turns it into fuel. He always has. So instead of crawling into bed and pulling the covers over his head, he booked a trip to Madrid to visit the Disc Jockeys family roots. The music. The heritage. The thing that had always been there when everything else fell apart.

He came back on fire. Ideas pouring out of him faster than he could write them down. A vinyl concept for Dubai. A brand that connected his father's legacy to the new world. Everyone around him—friends, family, well-meaning advisors—said the same thing: slow down. Too fast. You just got back. Think about it. Peter heard them, and he ignored them. He tried a pop-up in Dubai. It worked—sort of. People showed up. Records sold. The energy was good.

But when it was over, when the last crate was packed and the last customer had left, Peter stood in the empty space and felt nothing. Not satisfaction. Not excitement. Just a hollow echo where purpose should have been. "This is not what I want," he thought. He was lost again.

02
November 7, 2025

Dark Week

There is a week in every founder's story that they don't want to talk about. The week where the narrative stops being inspirational and starts being uncomfortably honest. For Peter, it was the week of November 7th.

Everything felt wrong. Not wrong in the way that a bad day feels wrong—wrong in the way that makes you question every decision you've ever made. He felt manipulated by people he had trusted. Used by people he had helped. The relationships he thought were solid turned out to be transactional, and when the transaction was over, so were the relationships. The phone stopped ringing. The messages stopped coming. The invitations dried up.

He was alone. Really, truly alone—except for Nour. She was there. She was always there. But even her presence couldn't fill the void that was opening up inside him, because this wasn't loneliness that another person could fix. This was the loneliness of realizing that the life you built doesn't belong to you.

He missed his dad's birthday. That one hurt the most. Kamal, the man who had spent fifty years keeping music alive in a country that kept trying to tear itself apart, turned another year older, and his son wasn't there. Peter couldn't find inspiration anywhere—not in work, not in people, not in the city that was supposed to be his launchpad. He sat with it. The emptiness. The silence. The weight of a week that felt like a year.

03
Week of Nov 3

The Spark

The frustration was everywhere now. It had seeped into every corner of his life like water through cracked concrete. Peter was angry—not at any one thing, but at the accumulation of things. Angry at friends who didn't show up. Angry at a professional world that rewarded politics over substance. Angry at himself for caring so much about people who clearly didn't care back. There was a deep loneliness underneath all of it, the kind that money and status and a nice apartment in Dubai can't touch. The kind that sits in your chest at 2am and whispers: "What are you actually doing with your life?"

Then a conversation happened—casual at first, the way all important conversations start—about AI versus the human world. About what happens when machines can do everything humans can do, except better and cheaper and without complaining. About what's left for people like Peter, who don't write code and don't build algorithms but who understand something that machines never will: taste. Curation. The feeling you get when a needle drops on a record that was pressed forty years ago in a factory that no longer exists. That conversation lodged itself in Peter's brain like a splinter. He couldn't shake it.

A few days later, he was in Lisbon. An ecosystem builder walking through a creative European city, seeing a different version of what life could look like. The narrow streets, the faded tiles, the record shops tucked into basements, the artists who lived on almost nothing but seemed richer than anyone Peter knew in Dubai. Something shifted. He started sketching an idea—a warehouse incubator, a creative hub, something he called Foundry OS. It was ambitious. It was exciting. It was too big.

He knew it was too big even as he wrote it down. The gap between the vision and his ability to execute it alone was a canyon. He needed something smaller. Something he could actually ship. Something that started with what he already had: his father's records, his own taste, and a stubborn refusal to work for someone else ever again.

"I need a purpose, a vision — not to follow money. I cannot be an employee anymore."

04
November 15, 2025

Pissed Off, Then Clarity

The loneliness didn't go away. It just changed shape. By mid-November it had stopped being the kind that makes you sad and started being the kind that makes you dangerous. Peter was done feeling sorry for himself. He was done waiting for the world to recognize what he was building. He was done asking for permission, for validation, for the approval of people who had never built anything themselves.

The anger was clean now. Focused. The kind of anger that doesn't destroy—it constructs. It builds walls and fills them with purpose. Peter looked at everything he had—Nour beside him, his father's legacy behind him, an idea that wouldn't stop growing inside him—and he made a decision that was less a business strategy and more a declaration of war.

"Peter + Nour vs everyone. Prove them wrong."

This was the moment the partnership crystallized. Not just the romantic partnership—that was already there, had been there through all the dark weeks and sleepless nights. This was something different. This was two people looking at each other and saying: we are going to build this thing together, and we are going to do it without anyone's help, and when it works—not if, when—we will remember every person who doubted us. Not for revenge. For fuel.

05
November 16, 2025

The Flight to SF

Peter boarded a plane to San Francisco the next day. Fourteen hours in the air. No meetings on the other end that mattered more than what was happening inside his head. He opened his laptop before the seatbelt sign turned off and started writing. Not notes. Not brainstorms. The actual deck. Word by word, slide by slide, the entire concept poured out of him like it had been waiting for permission.

Somewhere over the Atlantic, at 35,000 feet, with the cabin dark and the engine noise drowning out everything except his own thoughts, 80% of the concept crystallized. He could see it. Not the vague outline he'd been sketching in notebooks for weeks—the actual thing. The brand. The product. The model. The name. How it would feel to walk into the space. How it would feel to open the website. How it would feel to hold one of the records in your hands and know that it had traveled through fifty years of history to reach you.

He called it a visualization exercise. NODE ZERO. The starting point of everything. AI-enabled, not AI-dependent. Technology as a tool, not a personality. The machines would handle the logistics, the inventory, the SEO, the email flows. But the taste—the curation, the storytelling, the feeling—that would always be human. That would always be Kamal's legacy, filtered through Peter's obsession, amplified by Nour's eye.

"Real founder buys his freedom. No bosses, no big teams. An ecosystem loyal to your offerings."

By the time the plane touched down in San Francisco, Peter was a different person. Not because the city changed him—he barely saw it. Because the flight had given him the one thing he'd been missing for months: a plan he believed in completely. No hedging. No backup options. No "let me think about it." Full commitment. Execution mode on.

06
November 23–24, 2025

Post SF Reset

The high from San Francisco lasted about seventy-two hours. Then reality showed up, uninvited, and sat down across the table from Peter with a look that said: "So, how exactly are you going to do all of this?"

The grand vision—the AI-powered vinyl ecosystem, the cultural infrastructure play, the NODE ZERO concept that had felt so clear at 35,000 feet—still made sense. But it needed a starting point. A first step that wasn't a PowerPoint slide or a visualization exercise but an actual thing that real people could touch and buy and tell their friends about.

"RESET: pop up + ecomm. Do a good job, test setups, then go big."

Three words that don't sound revolutionary but were. Pop up. Ecomm. Test. Not "disrupt the music industry." Not "build the infrastructure for Arabic vinyl distribution across the GCC." Just: do a pop-up. Build a website. See if people actually want what you're selling. This was maturity. This was the visionary learning to be practical. The dreamer putting on work boots and picking up a hammer. Peter didn't abandon the big vision—he just stopped trying to build the penthouse before pouring the foundation.

07
November 25, 2025

The Internal Battle

This is the hardest chapter to write because it's about the thing that founders never talk about in their keynote speeches: the moment when you realize the problem might be you.

Peter was stuck in his own head. He was changing—rapidly, fundamentally, in ways that were visible to everyone around him. The person who had been comfortable in corporate settings, who knew how to navigate office politics and client dinners and the careful choreography of professional life, was dissolving. In his place, something harder was forming. Something with sharper edges. And those edges were cutting the people closest to him.

Relationships were fraying. Conversations that used to be easy became difficult. Friends who used to understand him started looking at him like he was speaking a different language. "Why is everyone wrong and I am right?" he asked himself. And then the gut punch of self-awareness that separates the founders who make it from the ones who don't: "I am the problem."

But here's the thing about Peter—self-awareness doesn't make him softer. It makes him harder. Not cruel. Not cold. Just more deliberate. He looked at the damage, acknowledged his part in it, and then made a decision that sounded cynical but was actually survival.

"Trust no one, hide your cards, be the vibe everyone wants. Trust NO ONE. GO."

It wasn't bitterness. It was strategy. Stop giving away the plan. Stop seeking validation from people who don't understand the vision. Stop trying to bring everyone along. Build it first. Show them the finished thing. Let the results speak.

08
Late Nov / Early Dec 2025

It Became Real

There is a moment in every startup's life that doesn't show up in the cap table or the pitch deck. It's the moment when the idea stops being an idea and becomes a physical thing that exists in the world. For Peter, it happened in the span of a few days at the end of November.

He hired an architect—someone from the Aya universe who understood that a record store isn't a retail space, it's a temple. They started talking about materials. About lighting. About the way a person should feel when they walk through the door. Not "browsing." Not "shopping." Arriving. Coming home to a place they didn't know they'd been missing.

Then he visited self.space. Then Effie Gallery. He stood in these places—spaces that other people had built with the same obsessive attention to feeling that Peter carried around like a second heartbeat—and something clicked. Not a new idea. Something deeper. A recognition. The abstract concept of "a record store in Dubai" became a room he could walk through in his mind. He could see the crates. He could see the walls. He could hear the music playing. He could smell the vinyl.

IT BECAME REAL. Not in the way that a business plan becomes real when you get funding, or a product becomes real when you ship it. Real in the way that matters most: Peter could feel it in his body. The nervous energy shifted from anxiety to anticipation. From "what if this doesn't work" to "I need to start building this immediately."

09
First Week of Dec 2025

Momentum

Gary Vee was in Dubai that week. You know the type—the hustle-culture preacher who talks about patience and execution and doing the boring work while everyone else is chasing shortcuts. Peter met him in the desert. Not at a conference. Not at a networking event. In the actual desert, where the sand meets the sky and everything feels both enormous and intimate at the same time.

Peter told him about the vision. About his dad. About fifty years of records and a bet on Arabic vinyl in a region that had forgotten its own musical heritage. Gary listened. Gary got it. And something about saying the idea out loud to someone who understood it—really understood it, not just nodded politely—made it feel more solid. More inevitable.

The trade license application started that week. A designer was hired. The energy was different now. Not manic, which was Peter's usual setting. Not desperate, which was where he'd been in November. Focused. Steady. Like a river that had found its channel and was no longer flooding the banks. Every day, something tangible happened. A form was filed. A mockup was approved. A conversation led to a connection that led to an opportunity.

"Feeling fucking inspired. It will be hard but easy. I am the one to do it with Nour."

10
Mid December — Greece

Full Build

The boring stuff started happening, and it was beautiful. Trade license. Bank account. Stripe integration. Branding decisions. Full website scope. E-commerce architecture. Crate designs. The kind of work that nobody puts on Instagram but that separates real businesses from mood boards. Peter plowed through it with the energy of someone who had finally found the thing worth doing the boring stuff for.

Daria quoted $6,300 for development and suggested an MVP approach. Samer had opinions. Everyone had opinions. Peter listened to all of them and filed them away in the part of his brain labeled "input, not instructions."

Then Greece. Peter flew to Athens with his dad. Two generations of record collectors walking through the narrow streets of a city that understands what it means to carry history forward. They found a shop—dusty, cramped, perfect—and bought 100 used vinyls. Father and son, flipping through crates together, holding up records and nodding at each other with the silent understanding of people who share the same obsession.

There is a photograph somewhere of Peter and Kamal in that Athens shop, surrounded by records, both of them smiling. It doesn't matter if the photo exists or not. The image is burned into the story. The old generation and the new one, standing in a foreign city, building the next chapter of something that started in Beirut fifty years ago. Peter felt confident. Clear. Happy. Three words that hadn't been in the same sentence for months.

11
3rd Week of Dec 2025

Listen to Your Gut

The developer finally put the scope together, and it was good work. Solid. Professional. The kind of document that makes a project feel real and terrifying at the same time, because now you can see exactly how much there is to build and how many ways it can go wrong.

Then the opinions started coming. Samer said do a lighter MVP. Strip it down. Don't over-build. The dev had a different vision. The designer had another. Nour had strong feelings about the direction, and those feelings didn't always align with Peter's, and when two people who love each other disagree about something they both care about deeply, the fights are nuclear. Not because they're mean. Because they're real.

Peter and Nour fought. Hard. About speed. About direction. About whether to listen to the advisors or trust their own instincts. About whether Peter was moving too fast or whether Nour was being too cautious. The kind of fights that leave you exhausted and raw and wondering if the partnership can survive the pressure of building something together.

And then, in the silence after one of those fights, the simplest and most powerful decision landed like a stone dropping into still water.

"Decided: listen to gut only."

No more committees. No more consensus. No more death by advisory. Peter had spent his entire career listening to other people—bosses, mentors, investors, friends who meant well but didn't have skin in the game. From this point forward, the gut decides. The gut that had been trained by forty years of life, by a father who built something from nothing, by the accumulated instinct of a thousand decisions made under pressure. Trust yourself. Ship it.

12
Christmas Week 2025

Disgusted, Then Inspired

Peter walked back into the office at Hub71 after the holiday break and felt something rise in his throat. Not nausea, exactly. Disgust. The fluorescent lights. The shared kitchen. The polite small talk with people building things he didn't care about. The entire choreography of corporate life that he had been performing for years, collecting a salary, pretending that stability was the same thing as satisfaction.

He sat at his desk and looked at his screen and thought: I am going to quit this job. Not today. But soon. And when I do, I'm never coming back to anything like it. The contrast between what he was building—this wild, personal, obsessive thing rooted in his family's history—and what he was doing for a paycheck was no longer bearable. The gap had become a canyon.

Christmas dinner that year turned into a pitch deck session. Somewhere between the main course and dessert, Peter pulled out his laptop and started showing Nour the latest version of the concept. She pushed back. Hard. They argued about positioning. They argued about timing. They argued about whether the brand voice should be warm or cool, loud or quiet, accessible or exclusive.

But something was different about these fights. They were productive. Each argument sharpened the idea rather than dulling it. By the end of the night, they had agreed on something more important than any brand guideline: the rules of engagement. They would be colleagues first. Clear agenda for every working conversation. No emotions in business decisions. Love each other at home. Challenge each other at work. It was the partnership agreement that no lawyer could have written.

13
Christmas – NYE 2025

Sri Lanka Reset

They flew to Sri Lanka for New Year's. Peter stepped off the plane into the humid air and felt something he hadn't felt in months: the absence of weight. Not happiness, exactly—that's too simple. The feeling of putting down a heavy bag you've been carrying so long you forgot it was there. Day one felt like rebirth. Not the dramatic, cinematic kind. The quiet kind. The kind where you wake up in a new place and your first thought isn't about work or money or the people who let you down. Your first thought is: I'm here. This is enough.

In that stillness, clarity came. Not the frantic, 3am-scribbling-in-a-notebook kind of clarity. The calm kind. Peter sat down and set the first real target: 1 million AED in revenue in 2026. 200,000 in profit. Real numbers. Not "disrupt the industry" numbers. Not "change the world" numbers. Survive-and-grow numbers. The kind of numbers that force you to do math instead of dreaming.

He committed to Vinyl Souk on February 1st and worked backwards. Every day between now and then had a task. Every task had a deadline. Every deadline was non-negotiable. The visionary in Peter—the one who wanted to build NODE ZERO and the Foundry and the AI-powered cultural infrastructure—didn't disappear. He just got put in a room with comfortable furniture and told to wait.

"Biggest learning: baby steps. Tame the visionary — keep him warm in the background."

This is where Peter the dreamer became Peter the builder. The difference between the two is not ambition. It's sequence. The dreamer wants everything at once. The builder wants the next brick.

14
January 2026

Ultimate Clarity

January was calm. Not the eerie calm before a storm—the real kind. The kind that comes after months of chaos when you finally stop fighting the current and start swimming with it. Peter woke up in the morning without the knot in his stomach. He went to sleep at night without the racing thoughts. For the first time in months, he was present. Not planning the future. Not regretting the past. Just here. Working.

The product had crystallized into something so simple it was almost embarrassing. Fixed crates. Simple credit subscription. That's it. No complex algorithms. No revolutionary technology. No paradigm shifts. Just: here are records, curated by people who have spent their entire lives loving music. Pick a crate. Subscribe if you want more. Everything else—the grand vision, the distribution play, the AI infrastructure—was still there, but it was in the background where it belonged.

Every day was prep for Vinyl Souk. Inventory. Pricing. Display. Packaging. The kind of work that looks boring from the outside but feels sacred when you're doing it for something you built yourself. Peter handled each record like his father handled them—with care, with knowledge, with the quiet confidence of someone who knows exactly what they're holding and why it matters.

15
February 2026

First Real Revenue

Vinyl Souk happened on February 1st. Peter set up the booth, arranged the crates, and waited. The first customer showed up early. Then another. Then another. By the end of the day, 15,000 AED had changed hands. Real money from real people who picked up real records and said real things like "Where has this been?" and "I've been looking for this for years" and "My mother used to play this when I was a kid."

Those words. Those specific words from strangers who had no reason to be kind. They landed differently than any investor pitch or advisory board compliment ever had. This wasn't validation from people who were paid to give opinions. This was validation from the market. From human beings reaching into their wallets because they wanted what Peter was selling. 15,000 AED. Not life-changing money. Life-confirming money.

But when the high wore off, reality reasserted itself. Nothing was ready to scale. The website wasn't built. The e-commerce flow didn't exist. Two agencies quoted 40 to 50 thousand dollars for development. Hamoom was stuck. Daria was stuck. Above the Fold wanted $50K. Peter stared at those numbers and felt the old frustration rising. He had proven the demand. He had proven the product. And now the thing standing between him and growth was a website he couldn't afford to build.

So he opened Replit and started building it himself. No development experience. No coding background. Just a founder who refused to let a price quote be the reason his dream stalled. Line by line, page by page, error by error, he built the website. Solo founder mentality. Figure it out or die trying.

"IT WORKED."

March 2026

AI Everything

The sign-ups crossed 100. Then the first order came in from someone Peter had never met, never spoken to, never shared a room with. A complete stranger, somewhere in the UAE, found the website, browsed the collection, picked a record, and paid for it. Then another stranger did the same thing. The system was working. The machine was running. And Peter hadn't slept properly in weeks because he was too busy being amazed that it was actually happening.

The packaging arrived. The printed materials. The branded crates. Every physical touchpoint that turned a transaction into an experience. Nour kept posting—daily content, relentless and beautiful, building the brand's visual language one frame at a time. The website was live and functional and imperfect and alive.

And then something happened that changed everything: Peter discovered that a MacBook Air, Claude, and a handful of Telegram bots could replace an entire team. Not theoretically. Actually. He built an AI workforce that would have cost six figures in salary if they were human. Leo handles operations—inventory tracking, order management, the daily logistics of running a business. Luna handles content and marketing, feeding Nour's creative direction into campaigns and email flows. Rami handles SEO, building the foundation that would make UF findable. Scout watches the market, scanning Reddit and forums for conversations about Arabic vinyl. Atlas runs the Sunday CEO brief, synthesizing a week of data into decisions. Kamal—named after Peter's father—curates the music, organizing 188,870 SKUs into 20 moods that map the emotional landscape of Arabic music.

"I move so fast people get overwhelmed — AI fixed that. Now I have a team."

Six AI agents. Zero employees. The infrastructure of a funded startup running on a single MacBook from a guy who learned to code three months ago. It wasn't perfect. The blog email incident proved that—an accidental send to 56 subscribers that taught Peter the price of moving fast without guardrails. But the lesson was absorbed, the guardrails were built, and the machine kept running. Faster now. Smarter. More dangerous.

17
April 2026

Breaking Through

Everything converged in April. Nour started posting daily—not just content, but a visual manifesto for what UF stood for. TikTok went live. The brand started showing up in places Peter hadn't put it, shared by people he'd never met, discussed in conversations he wasn't part of. The organic growth that every founder prays for but can't manufacture was happening. Slowly at first, then all at once.

The pop-up at Shabab Lab was supposed to be another test. Another data point. Another "let's see if this works." It turned into the best day of Peter's professional life. Fifty-five customers walked in. Not browsers. Buyers. People who came with intention and left with records and came back with friends. AED 22,166 in a single day. More than some stores make in a week. More than Peter had dared to project. The energy in that room was electric—strangers bonding over music, flipping through crates, discovering artists they'd never heard of, rediscovering artists they'd forgotten. This was it. This was the vision made physical.

Then, at 3am, Peter's phone buzzed. An email from a stranger—someone who had bought records at the pop-up just hours earlier. The tone was hostile. The message was a threat. This person claimed to be an exclusive distributor for certain labels in the region and made it clear that Peter was stepping on territory that didn't belong to him. Legal action was implied. Boundaries were drawn. The message was designed to make Peter afraid.

Most founders would have panicked. Called a lawyer. Lost sleep. Second-guessed everything. Peter read the email twice, put his phone down, and went to sleep. In the morning, he didn't respond to the threat. Instead, he did something that revealed more about who he is than any pitch deck or brand manifesto ever could: he emailed the labels directly. Wewantsounds. Indigo. Souma Records. Habibi Funk. If someone was going to be the exclusive distributor for Arabic vinyl in the Gulf, it was going to be him. Not the stranger sending threatening emails at 3am. Him.

"Don't say it. Become it."

A new agent was created that week: Elie, the distribution agent. Named, like all of Peter's AI team, with intention. Elie's job was singular and relentless: reach out to every label that mattered, build the relationships, secure the deals, and turn UF from a retailer into the infrastructure layer for Arabic music in the Gulf. Thirty days to own the market. The clock started ticking. May 14 finale at Shabab Lab—not just a pop-up anymore, but a statement.

84 orders. AED 46,649 revenue. From zero to market position in four months.

18
Now

What's Next

"We're not building a record store. We're building the infrastructure for Arabic music in the Gulf."

Read that sentence again. Because it's not marketing copy. It's not a tagline designed to impress investors. It's the truth of what UF has become in five months—from a dark week in November where Peter couldn't get out of his own head, to a company that is actively reshaping how Arabic music reaches the people who love it.

84 orders. AED 46,649 in revenue. 22,170 pages indexing on Google. Monthly pop-ups locked. Distribution partnerships in progress with the labels that matter. An AI team of six agents running operations that would require a dozen humans. A content engine powered by Nour's relentless creative eye. A supply chain built on 188,870 SKUs and a relationship with the oldest vinyl collection in the Middle East.

But the numbers don't tell the real story. The real story is the Disc Jockey heritage—fifty years of Kamal's obsessive curation, his refusal to let Arabic music disappear into the digital void, his belief that a vinyl record is not a product but a vessel for something sacred. That heritage isn't just a brand story. It's the unfair advantage that no competitor can replicate. You can copy a website. You can undercut a price. You can even mimic a brand voice. But you cannot manufacture fifty years of love for music. You cannot fake the knowledge that comes from handling 90,000 records one by one, year after year, war after war, generation after generation.

Peter took that heritage, combined it with the most powerful AI tools on the planet, wrapped it in a brand that speaks quietly but carries weight, and built something that didn't exist five months ago. He did it with Nour. He did it without a team. He did it while working a full-time job he was planning to quit. He did it after the darkest weeks of his life, after the loneliness and the self-doubt and the 3am emails from strangers trying to scare him off.
